Classification Framework

Definitions and interpretation guidelines for index classifications.

Framework Overview

The classification framework provides a standardised method for categorising businesses based on the interpretability of their public digital signals by automated systems.

This framework uses three mutually exclusive classifications. Each business is assigned exactly one classification based on assessment at a point in time.

Classification reflects interpretability of public signals by automated systems. It is not a measure of quality, reputation, or compliance.

Classifications

Clear

Definition

Public signals allow consistent automated interpretation. The business is reliably identifiable and categorisable by AI systems.

Characteristics

  • • Business information is consistent across multiple public sources
  • • Service descriptions are unambiguous and machine-readable
  • • Location data is structured and verifiable
  • • Third-party references align with stated business information
Ambiguous

Definition

Signals exist but introduce uncertainty or misclassification risk. The business may be inconsistently represented across platforms.

Characteristics

  • • Some inconsistencies exist in business information across sources
  • • Service descriptions may be incomplete or unclear
  • • Location data may be present but with conflicting details
  • • Third-party references show partial alignment
Uninterpretable

Definition

Signals are insufficient for reliable automated interpretation. The business may be overlooked or misrepresented by AI systems.

Characteristics

  • • Limited or no consistent business information across public sources
  • • Service descriptions are absent, vague, or contradictory
  • • Location data is missing or unverifiable
  • • Few or no third-party references available

Interpretation Guidelines

Classifications should be interpreted within the following context:

  • Point-in-time: Classifications reflect the state of signals at the time of assessment. Signals may change.
  • Interpretability only: A "Clear" classification does not mean a business is better than one classified as "Ambiguous".
  • No causation: Classification does not predict or guarantee any specific outcome in AI systems.
  • Context-dependent: The relevance of interpretability varies by sector and discovery context.
